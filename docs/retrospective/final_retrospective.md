Reflecting on our last iteration, and comparing it to our first srs document we noticed the difference and also similarities that our project ended up taking course with. Our original SRS had some big ideas that included natural language strategy input, AI powered backtesting, plain English to strategy translation. Somewhere around Iteration 2 we took a step back and scoped down to something we could actually ship in the time given and what would actually make sense for the purpose of our application and its use cases in the field that it is in. We finalized with a simulated trading platform with real market data, portfolio tracking, quizzes, and social competition. Upon actually implementing, receieving feedback and many pivots, we were able to get realistic, and the product we ended up with is better because of it.

Looking back across all five iterations, the some things took a few tries to correct. We underestimated how long things took, especially API integration and getting everything working together rather than just in isolation. Merge conflicts snowballed every time we left things too late. But by the last few iteration, we learned how to work better as a team. To not only come up with cool elaborate ideas but to slipt up tasks and bring it together for a functionalities that we would all be proud of. 

By Iteration 5, with all our must-haves done and a solid handful of nice-to-haves implemented too, we finally had space to actually slow down. We got feedback, polished things properly, and sat with the question of whether InvestEd actually does what we wanted it to do. It does. A Hopkins student who knows nothing about trading can open the app, place simulated trades with live data, watch their portfolio move, test their knowledge, and compete on a leaderboard. 

If we're being honest about what we'd do differently, it comes down to a few things: scope tighter, be more strict with what features actually mattered and what just sounded cool at the time. Our application reflected technical growth but more important we've all grown as developers, learned to PR more often, to discusses next steps, and to listen and reflect as a team. We are grateful for the experiences and lessons that our project presented us. Thank you Professor Darvish and our CA for helping to guide us through our many iterations and hiccups.
